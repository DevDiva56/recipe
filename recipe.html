My Favorite Recipe

GRANDMA'S FAMOUS PILAU

"The kitchen was always the warmest place in Grandma’s house.

Not just because of the firewood stove or the steam that clung to the walls — but because of the stories that came with every meal.

And on special days, when the house began to smell like toasted spices and browned onions, we all knew one thing: Grandma was making pilau. She never called it “a recipe.” To her, it was a memory she could cook."

Ingredients

  • Rice (2 cups, she never measured exactly — “just enough for love,” she said)
  • Beef or goat meat (optional but traditional)
  • Onions (lots — “onions are the heart of pilau”)
  • Garlic and ginger paste
  • Pilau masala (or whole spices: cardamom, cloves, cinnamon, cumin)
  • Tomatoes (optional in some homes, but she liked a little)
  • Potatoes (her secret — they “stretch blessings”)
  • Salt to taste
  • Water or broth

Her method (which was more ritual than cooking)

  1. Brown the onions slowly

    She would not allow rushing here. “If onions don’t cry properly, your pilau will taste shy,” she joked.

  2. Add garlic, ginger, and spices

    The moment this hit the pan, she would pause. “This is where the spirit enters,” she’d say.

  3. Add meat and let it soak in the flavor

    No stirring in a hurry. Just patience.

  4. Add potatoes

    “So nobody leaves the table still hungry,” she’d explain.

  5. Add rice and water

    This was the moment she became serious. The spoon slowed. The kitchen quieted.

  6. Let it cook without disturbance

    “If you keep checking it, it will punish you,” she’d warn.

The real ingredient

The truth was, her pilau never tasted the same when anyone else tried to make it. We followed her steps exactly — but something was missing. Years later, I realized what it was. It wasn’t the spices.

It was the way she cooked while remembering people. Every stir had a name attached to it. Every simmer carried a story. Every serving was an act of love disguised as food.

And when she finally served it — steaming, fragrant, golden rice on worn plates — she would always say the same thing:

“Eat. Food is better when it is shared.”
